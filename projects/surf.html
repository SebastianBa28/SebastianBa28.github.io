Research Overview
Conducted research in fluid dynamics under Professor Mory Gharib's Research Group. Led experimental study of vortex formation times across perforated plates for micro-aerial vehicles, using fluorescent dye visualization and Particle Imaging Velocimetry (PIV).

Vortex Rings

Vortex rings are flow structures characterized by rotating fluid motion where the flow curls around a vortex core. They appear in ocean currents, bird flight, and experimental fluid modeling. Gharib (1998) identified a universal vortex formation timescale of 3.6–4.5 at which a vortex achieves maximum circulation before pinch-off — a ratio of stroke distance to cylinder diameter.

Experiment Overview

We explored the wake dynamics of perforated plates at varying porosity levels. By allowing fluid to flow through the plate surface, perforated plates display different aerodynamic properties than solid plates — affecting drag, lift, and boundary layer behavior. Three porosity ratios were tested:

  • 63% Open Area
  • 33% Open Area
  • 0% Open Area (solid)

Experimental Setup

A frame was designed and manufactured to mount the perforated plates onto a Captive Trajectory System, translating them across a large water tank.

Flow Dye Visualization

Fluorescent dye under UV light was used to observe vortex formation as each perforated plate moved through the water.

Particle Imaging Velocimetry (PIV)

Highly-reflective particles were illuminated by a high-power laser and imaged by a camera. Image-processing techniques generated a velocity vector field to determine the direction and magnitude of fluid motion around each plate.

Vortex Circulation Analysis

From the velocity field, the vorticity field was computed to identify vortex formation and shedding. Circulation was calculated to assess vortex strength and determine the formation number.

Results

Perforated plates formed vortices significantly earlier than the solid plate:

  • Solid plate: formation number at T = 6.8
  • 33% Open Area: peak circulation at T = 3.3
  • 63% Open Area: peak circulation at T = 2.3

This behavior is attributed to jets flowing across the perforation area and their interaction with the trailing wake.
